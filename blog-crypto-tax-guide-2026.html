Crypto Tax Guide 2026: How to Report & Minimize Taxes

Updated March 3, 2026 · 17 min read · by 13.Markets

Cryptocurrency taxes in the United States have become significantly clearer in 2026, but also more strictly enforced. The IRS now receives transaction reports directly from centralized exchanges (1099-DA forms became mandatory in 2025), and blockchain analytics tools make it easier than ever for the government to track on-chain activity.

The good news: with proper planning and the right tools, you can accurately report your crypto taxes and legally minimize your tax burden. This guide covers everything you need to know for the 2025 tax year (filed in 2026).

How Crypto Is Taxed in 2026

The IRS classifies cryptocurrency as property, not currency. This means every disposal of crypto — selling, trading, or spending — is potentially a taxable event that triggers capital gains or losses.

Short-term gains (assets held less than 12 months) are taxed at your ordinary income tax rate, which can be as high as 37% for high earners. Long-term gains (assets held over 12 months) benefit from preferential rates: 0% for incomes up to $47,025 (single), 15% for most taxpayers, and 20% for those earning above $518,900 (single).

Taxable Events

Not every crypto transaction triggers taxes. Here is a clear breakdown:

Taxable (You Owe Taxes)

  • Selling crypto for USD: The difference between your sale price and cost basis is a capital gain or loss.
  • Trading crypto for crypto: Swapping BTC for ETH is a taxable disposal of BTC. Your gain/loss is calculated on the BTC sold.
  • Spending crypto on goods/services: Using BTC to buy a product triggers a capital gain/loss on the BTC spent.
  • Earning crypto income: Mining rewards, staking rewards, airdrops, and DeFi yield are taxed as ordinary income at fair market value when received.
  • Getting paid in crypto: Salary or freelance payments in crypto are ordinary income, just like receiving USD.

Not Taxable (No Tax Due)

  • Buying crypto with USD: Simply purchasing and holding is not taxable.
  • Transferring between your own wallets: Moving crypto from Coinbase to your Ledger is not a taxable event.
  • Gifting crypto (under $18,000): Annual gift exclusion applies. Above that, it counts against your lifetime estate tax exemption.
  • Donating crypto to charity: Donating appreciated crypto can actually provide a tax deduction at fair market value.

DeFi-Specific Tax Rules

DeFi Tax Complexity

DeFi creates the most complex tax situations in crypto. Each protocol interaction can generate multiple taxable events. Automated tax software is essentially required for active DeFi users.

  • Token swaps (Uniswap, etc.): Every swap is a taxable trade. ETH to USDC on Uniswap = disposal of ETH.
  • Liquidity providing: Adding liquidity to an AMM pool is generally treated as a taxable trade of your tokens for LP tokens. Removing liquidity is another taxable event.
  • Yield farming rewards: Tokens earned from farming are ordinary income at the time of receipt. When you later sell those tokens, any price change is a capital gain/loss.
  • Staking rewards: Following IRS guidance, staking rewards are income when received (or when you gain dominion and control over them).
  • Wrapping/unwrapping tokens: The IRS has not provided clear guidance, but most tax software treats wrapping (ETH to WETH) as non-taxable. Cross-chain bridges are less clear.
  • NFT sales: Selling an NFT triggers capital gains on the difference between sale price and your cost basis (mint price + gas).

New for 2026: Form 1099-DA

Starting with the 2025 tax year, centralized exchanges like Coinbase, Kraken, and Gemini are required to issue Form 1099-DA to both you and the IRS. This form reports your gross proceeds from crypto sales, similar to how brokerages report stock trades on Form 1099-B.

This is a significant change. Previously, exchanges issued 1099-MISC or 1099-K inconsistently. Now the IRS has a clear picture of your centralized exchange activity. However, 1099-DA does not cover on-chain DeFi activity or wallet-to-wallet transfers — you are still responsible for tracking and reporting those yourself.

Tax Minimization Strategies (Legal)

1. Tax-Loss Harvesting

Sell crypto positions that are at a loss to offset capital gains from winners. Unlike stocks, crypto is not subject to the wash sale rule (as of 2025 tax year), meaning you can sell at a loss and immediately rebuy the same asset. This is the single most powerful legal tax reduction strategy for crypto holders.

2. Hold for Over 12 Months

Long-term capital gains rates (0-20%) are dramatically lower than short-term rates (10-37%). If you can wait 12 months before selling, you could cut your tax rate in half or more.

3. Use Specific Identification (HIFO)

When selling crypto, you can choose which specific lots to sell. The HIFO (Highest In, First Out) method sells your highest-cost lots first, minimizing your capital gain. Most tax software supports this automatically.

4. Donate Appreciated Crypto

Donating crypto that has appreciated in value to a qualified charity lets you deduct the full fair market value without paying capital gains tax on the appreciation. This is more tax-efficient than selling the crypto and donating cash.

5. Contribute to a Self-Directed IRA

Several providers now offer self-directed IRAs that can hold cryptocurrency. Contributions may be tax-deductible (traditional IRA) or grow tax-free (Roth IRA), sheltering your crypto gains from taxes entirely.

6. Relocate to a Tax-Friendly State

States like Florida, Texas, Wyoming, and Nevada have no state income tax, meaning your crypto gains are only taxed at the federal level. For large portfolios, the savings can be substantial.
